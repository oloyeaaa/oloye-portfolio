When I scanned 1,256 live UK MarTech and Salesforce job ads this month, I expected the top skill to be a platform name. HubSpot, maybe. Or "Salesforce Administrator."

It wasn't. The top skill was **stakeholder management** — mentioned in 41 ads, ahead of every tool, cert, and technical framework.

That's not a rounding error. That's the market telling you what it actually wants.

## Why it ranks so high

MarTech and Salesforce roles sit in the middle of an organisation. You usually report to marketing, but you build for sales. You implement tools paid for by finance. You're governed by legal and IT. And you run processes that directly affect commercial revenue.

That means your real job — the one they're paying for — isn't configuring Salesforce. It's getting four groups of people who don't speak the same language to agree on what to configure.

Trainers don't talk about this because it's not teachable in a module. But it's the skill that gets people promoted, and the absence of it is what keeps people stuck.

## The mistake I see on CVs

Most candidates transitioning in from a non-tech role already have stakeholder skills — they've just learned to hide them. They strip "coordinated with ten suppliers," "chaired weekly reviews with senior leadership," "mediated between teams with opposing priorities" out of their CV, because they assume those don't count once they're chasing a "technical" job.

They count more than the tools do.

## How to show stakeholder skills with no direct MarTech experience

Three moves:

**1. Reframe your old roles.** If you led a retail team, you managed stakeholders every day — customers, head office, suppliers. Write the line as:

> *Managed cross-functional alignment across store operations, area management, and supplier accounts to deliver weekly trading outcomes.*

Same job. Different language. And it's not spin — it's accurate.

**2. Build one project that requires external input.** In your Salesforce dev org, don't build a toy CRM. Build something for a real person — a local charity, a side hustle, a friend's business. Then document the bit where you had to explain tradeoffs to a non-technical user. That conversation *is* the skill.

**3. Prepare one concrete story.** In every MarTech interview I've sat in on, the stakeholder question comes up in some form: "Tell me about a time you had to deliver something when people disagreed." Have one clean story. STAR format. Two minutes. Rehearsed until it's natural but not robotic.

## What it looks like in practice

Most of my week at work isn't in Salesforce. It's in Teams calls, answering questions like *"why can't we just send the email now?"* with a calm explanation of why the data isn't clean yet. Then following up with a written summary so the same question doesn't get asked twice next week.

That's the job. The tools are the easy part.

## The reframe

If you're coming from outside tech and worrying about your lack of certifications — you already have the harder half. The tooling can be learned in 90 days. The instincts to keep five people from derailing a project? That took you ten years of real work.

Don't bury it.

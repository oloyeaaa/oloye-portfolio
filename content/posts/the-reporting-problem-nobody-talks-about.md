I've sat in hundreds of marketing meetings where someone presents a dashboard full of metrics and the room nods along. Open rates. Click rates. MQLs. Pipeline influenced.

Then someone asks: "So what should we do differently?" And the room goes quiet.

That's the reporting problem nobody talks about. We're measuring everything and learning nothing.

## Vanity Metrics vs. Decision Metrics

A vanity metric makes you feel good. A decision metric tells you what to do next.

**Vanity:** "We generated 500 MQLs this month."
**Decision:** "MQLs from webinars convert to pipeline at 3x the rate of gated content MQLs, and they close 40% faster."

The first tells you volume. The second tells you where to invest.

## Building a Decision-First Dashboard

Here's the framework I use:

### 1. Start with the Decision

What decisions does this report need to inform? "Should we increase investment in Channel X?" is a decision. "How many leads did we get?" is not.

### 2. Identify the Leading Indicators

Revenue is a lagging indicator. By the time you see it in a report, the actions that caused it happened months ago. Find the leading indicators — the metrics that predict future revenue, not just record past activity.

### 3. Add Context, Not Just Numbers

A number without context is meaningless. "500 MQLs" could be great or terrible depending on your target, your historical average, and the quality of those leads.

Every metric in your dashboard should show: current value, target, trend, and the "so what?"

## The Weekly Review

The best marketing ops teams I've seen run a 30-minute weekly review. Not a status update — a decision meeting. They look at three things:

1. What changed this week?
2. What does that mean?
3. What are we going to do about it?

If your reporting doesn't enable that conversation, it's decoration, not intelligence.

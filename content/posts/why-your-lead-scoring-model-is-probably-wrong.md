Most lead scoring models I've inherited share the same problem: they score activity, not intent.

Someone downloads three whitepapers? 30 points. Visits the pricing page once? 10 points. But the whitepaper downloader might be a student writing a dissertation, while the pricing page visitor is a VP with budget approval ready to go.

## The Activity Trap


![Intricate abstract 3D render of white geometric shapes, perfect for modern backgrounds.](/images/blog/why-your-lead-scoring-model-is-probably-wrong-1.jpg)

*Photo by Steve A Johnson on Pexels*
Traditional lead scoring treats every interaction as equal. Open an email? Points. Click a link? Points. Attend a webinar? Lots of points. But volume of engagement doesn't correlate with readiness to buy.

The data backs this up. When I audited one company's scoring model against their actual closed-won deals, only 23% of their "marketing qualified" leads had any of the top-scoring behaviours in their model.

## What to Score Instead


![Top-down view of a laptop keyboard and colorful printed charts on a desk.](/images/blog/why-your-lead-scoring-model-is-probably-wrong-2.jpg)

*Photo by Lukas Blazek on Pexels*
Here's what I've started building into scoring models:

1. **Intent signals** — pricing page visits, competitor comparison searches, case study downloads
2. **Fit signals** — company size, industry, tech stack (matched against your ICP)
3. **Timing signals** — recent funding, new hires in relevant roles, contract renewal windows
4. **Negative signals** — competitor employees, students, job seekers

## The Rebuild Process

Strip your model back to basics. Look at your last 50 closed-won deals and reverse-engineer the actual journey. Which pages did they visit? What content did they engage with? How long was the cycle?

Build from evidence, not assumptions. Your CRM has the data — you just need to look at it honestly.

The best scoring model isn't the most complex one. It's the one that actually predicts revenue.
